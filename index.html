  • Aurras: Open-Field Active Noise Cancellation on an FPGA

    This project introduces **Aurras**, a lightweight FPGA-based system designed for open-field noise cancellation. The system dynamically reduces environmental noise by using impulse-based calibration to adapt to the acoustics of any indoor environment. It processes sound via microphones and speakers, using convolution and delay techniques to generate "anti-noise" signals that interfere destructively with unwanted sound waves. It is implemented on a Spartan-7 FPGA, optimized for low memory and computational use, and achieves significant noise reduction across a broad frequency range, especially between 500-900 Hz.

  • Multi-Modal Person Re-Identification

    This research focuses on enhancing person re-identification (re-ID) by combining both visual and textual data. Traditional re-ID systems often struggle with challenges like lighting variations, occlusions, and pose changes. To overcome these, I introduce a multimodal approach using the CLIP model, integrating image captions generated by the mPLUG-Owl2 model. This fusion of visual and textual data aims to improve the accuracy and robustness of re-ID systems.

  • Event-Based Contrastive Learning for Medical Time Series

    This paper introduces Event-Based Contrastive Learning (EBCL), a method designed to improve predictive modeling for medical time series data. EBCL focuses on learning embeddings that capture patient-specific temporal trends around key medical events, such as hospital admissions. By contrasting data from before and after these critical events, the model significantly enhances performance on downstream tasks, including predicting 30-day readmission, 1-year mortality, and 1-week length of stay. The method was tested on a cohort of heart failure patients and showed superior results compared to other pretraining models, highlighting its potential to inform clinical decision-making and patient care. This paper was accepted to MLHC 2024.
